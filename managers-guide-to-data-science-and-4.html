Manager's Guide to Data Science and Machine Learning - Part 4

published Date: Aug 2023 |  4 minutes read

Machine learning is a vast and dynamic field with various algorithms and techniques. One crucial way to classify these methods is based on the input-output relationship, particularly focusing on the probability model. The two main categories in this context are Discriminative and Generative models.

Discriminative Models

Discriminative methods aim to differentiate between different classes of data. These models learn the probability of an output given an input. For instance, in an image classification task where you need to identify whether an image contains a cat or a dog, a discriminative model predicts the likelihood of the image being a cat versus a dog. It acts as a forward model, making predictions based on input data.

A classic example of a discriminative model is Logistic Regression. This is used for binary classification tasks, where the goal is to predict one of two outcomes. For example, in email classification, logistic regression can predict whether an email is spam (1) or not spam (0) based on features like email length and the number of exclamation marks.

Other discriminative models include Support Vector Machines (SVMs), traditional neural networks, nearest neighbor algorithms, Conditional Random Fields (CRFs), and decision trees like Random Forests. Discriminative models don't assume specific properties of the data points; instead, they focus on distinguishing one class from another.

However, discriminative models cannot generate entirely new data points. They are designed to make predictions based on existing data rather than creating novel instances.

Generative Models

In contrast, generative models seek to model the joint probability of both input (x) and output (y), or sometimes the reverse probability. Rather than merely predicting outcomes, they aim to understand the underlying distribution of the data and generate new data samples that resemble the training data.

For example, if you have a dataset of 1D data points, such as people's heights in a population, generative models can create new data points that closely resemble the height distribution in the dataset.

Common types of generative models include Naïve Bayes, Bayesian networks, Markov Random Fields, Hidden Markov Models (HMMs), Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA), and the popular Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs).

Discriminative models make predictions on unseen data based on conditional probabilities and are often used for classification or regression problems. They excel at distinguishing between classes. On the other hand, generative models focus on understanding the data distribution and can generate new, similar data points. They are valuable when you need to create new data that fits a particular pattern.

Using the right model for any AI projects, depends on the specific task. For classification and distinguishing between data points, discriminative models are ideal. For tasks requiring data generation or understanding the overall data distribution, generative models are the way to go. This balance between prediction and data generation is crucial in deploying AI solutions effectively.
