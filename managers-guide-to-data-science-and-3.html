Manager's Guide to Data Science and Machine Learning - Part 3

published Date: Aug 2023 |  6 minutes read

Machine learning has revolutionized how we tackle complex problems, predict outcomes, and extract insights from data. A key decision in machine learning is choosing between parametric and non-parametric models. These two categories differ in their approach to handling data and have unique strengths and weaknesses. In this post, we’ll explore parametric and non-parametric models, highlighting their differences and when to use each.

Parametric Models: Structure and Assumptions

Parametric models have a predefined structure and assumptions about the relationship between input and output variables. They express this relationship using a fixed formula. Key features of parametric models include:

  1. Fixed Number of Parameters: These models have a set number of parameters or coefficients, which are learned from the training data. For example, in linear regression, the model learns the slope and intercept to fit a line to the data.

  2. Assumed Relationship: Parametric models assume a specific functional form for the relationship. For instance, linear regression assumes a linear relationship, while logistic regression assumes a linear relationship between the input and the log-odds of an event, predicting the probability of binary outcomes, such as employee turnover.

  3. Limited Flexibility: Due to their predefined structure, parametric models struggle with complex and nonlinear relationships. For example, using a linear regression model to predict employee salary based on experience might work well for simple, linear relationships but fail when the relationship is more intricate.

An illustrative example is linear regression, which assumes a linear relationship between input (years of experience) and output (salary), aiming to find the best-fit line by adjusting coefficients to minimize prediction errors.

Non-Parametric Models: Adaptability and Flexibility

Non-parametric models take a different approach, not making strong assumptions about the relationship between variables. They rely on the data’s inherent structure. Key characteristics include:

  1. Variable Model Complexity: The complexity depends on the training samples. Non-parametric models adapt to various data patterns, making them suitable for tasks where relationships are complex and not easily defined by a formula.

  2. No Assumed Relationship: They do not impose a specific relationship between input and output, allowing them to capture complex and nonlinear relationships in the data.

  3. Local Structure: Non-parametric models, like the k-nearest neighbors (KNN) algorithm, make predictions based on local data structures. For example, KNN predicts an employee’s salary by comparing it to the salaries of employees with similar experience and qualifications.

Consider KNN for grouping fruits: it doesn't assume a specific relationship between features (like weight and color) and the fruit type. Instead, it looks at the closest data points to determine the class of a new input, making it adaptable to various data distributions.

Choosing Between Parametric and Non-Parametric Models

  1. Nature of the Data: If the relationship is simple and well-understood, parametric models work well. For complex, non-linear relationships, non-parametric models are often better.

  2. Amount of Data: Parametric models are efficient with large datasets, requiring fewer parameters. Non-parametric models might struggle with high-dimensional or large datasets.

  3. Interpretability: Parametric models are more interpretable due to their predefined structure. Non-parametric models rely on data-driven patterns, which can be harder to interpret.

  4. Computational Resources: Non-parametric models can be computationally intensive, especially with many neighbors in KNN. Parametric models generally offer faster predictions.

In summary, choosing between parametric and non-parametric models is crucial in machine learning. Parametric models bring predefined assumptions, making them suitable for certain scenarios. Non-parametric models provide flexibility and adaptability, capturing complex relationships effectively.
